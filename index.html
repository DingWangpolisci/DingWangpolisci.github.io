Research: Local context, housing, and policy preferences

One strand of my research examines how subnational economic contexts—especially housing affordability, zoning regimes, and localized inequality—shape attitudes toward redistribution, immigration, and welfare policy. I argue that economic pressures are experienced locally and that individuals often interpret national debates through the lens of their immediate environments, with housing functioning as a central mechanism linking economic strain to intergroup attitudes and perceptions of deservingness and blame.

Methods: I use geocoded survey data linked to administrative indicators and local policy environments, combining multilevel modeling and quasi-experimental strategies with survey experiments to estimate contextual effects and heterogeneity.

Research: Gender, evaluation, and accountability

A second strand of my research focuses on gender and politics, emphasizing the conditional nature of stereotyping, evaluation, and accountability. Rather than treating bias as fixed, I examine how gendered expectations are activated by informational, institutional, and political–economic contexts, shaping candidate evaluation and differential accountability—often intersecting with broader inequality dynamics.

Methods: This work integrates survey experiments and conjoint designs with large-scale observational data to identify when stereotypes become politically consequential and how contextual signals moderate voter judgment.

Teaching

I have taught and assisted courses in political behavior, American politics, comparative politics, and research methods. My teaching emphasizes applying empirical evidence to evaluate substantive claims, helping students connect research design and measurement to real political debates.

I focus on methodological literacy, critical engagement with evidence, and inclusive classroom practices that support students from diverse backgrounds and preparation levels.
