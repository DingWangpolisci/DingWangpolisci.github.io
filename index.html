Research: Local economic context and policy preferences

One strand of my research examines how subnational economic contexts— especially labor market shocks, housing affordability and land-use regulation, and localized inequality—shape attitudes toward immigration and welfare policy. I argue that economic pressures are experienced locally and that individuals interpret national debates through the lens of their immediate environments, with perceived group status threat serving as a central mechanism linking economic strain to intergroup attitudes and perceptions of deservingness and blame.

Methods: Geocoded survey data linked to administrative indicators and local policy text; multilevel modeling; quasi-experimental designs; and survey experiments to estimate contextual effects and heterogeneity.

Research: Gender stereotypes and candidate evaluation

A second strand of my research focuses on gender and politics, emphasizing how stereotyping conditionally shapes political evaluation and accountability, often in ways that vary by group identity. Rather than treating bias as fixed, I examine how gendered expectations are activated by informational, institutional, and political–economic contexts.

Methods: Survey experiments, conjoint designs, and large-scale observational data to identify when stereotypes become politically consequential and how contextual signals moderate voter judgment.

Teaching
